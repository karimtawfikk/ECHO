Ramesses II, known as Ramesses the Great, was the third pharaoh of the nineteenth Dynasty and ruled Egypt for a remarkable sixty-six years. Ascending the throne at age twenty, he became one of the most influential leaders in history, born to Seti I and Queen Tuya. He began his reign by expanding Egypt's borders and securing vital trade routes across the region.

His early rule was defined by a fierce conflict with the Hittites, culminating in the famous Battle of Kadesh. Despite being misled by spies and nearly annihilated, Ramesses rallied his troops and fought to a stalemate. This historic struggle eventually led to the world’s earliest known peace treaty, signed in 1258 BCE.

Beyond the battlefield, Ramesses was a prolific builder who transformed the Egyptian landscape. He constructed the magnificent royal capital of Per-Ramesses and finished his father’s grand projects at Karnak and Abydos. Most famously, he commissioned the colossal rock-cut temples of Abu Simbel, featuring four massive sixty-foot statues of himself.

Ramesses fathered over one hundred sons and sixty daughters, including his successor Merneptah. He honored his favorite wife, Nefertari, with a grand temple and a beautifully decorated tomb in the Valley of the Queens. While modern films often link him to the biblical Exodus, there is no historical evidence in Egyptian records to support this association.

Reigning until nearly age ninety, Ramesses lived long enough to celebrate multiple festivals of rejuvenation. Upon his death in 1213 BCE, he left behind an empire at the peak of its imperial power. Today, his mummy remains preserved in Cairo, serving as a lasting testament to the most revered ancestor of ancient Egypt.
